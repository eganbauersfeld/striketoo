Our first prototype is a forearm gauntlet â€” a rigid sleeve that mounts to the forearm and holds a racket attachment, removing the need for the student to grip the implement at all. The gauntlet is modeled after AFO (Ankle-Foot Orthosis) bracing principles: a form-fitting shell that distributes load across the limb rather than concentrating it at the hand.

What Sarah Liked

Sarah was immediately receptive to the concept of a forearm-mounted attachment. Removing the grip requirement entirely addresses the core challenge her students face, and she appreciated that the gauntlet could work for students across a wide range of hand mobility levels.

What Needed to Change

When Egan demonstrated the prototype by wrapping it around his forearm, Sarah noticed that his hand was bent into an awkward dorsiflexed position. She pointed out that most of her students would be physically unable to hold their hands in that orientation â€” and for those who could, it would be uncomfortable and potentially harmful over time.

Her Suggestions

Sarah recommended that the gauntlet be redesigned around a neutral hand position â€” the natural resting position of the hand when the arm hangs relaxed at the side, with no forced extension or flexion of the wrist. She also flagged that some of her students cannot open their hands at all, meaning any design needs a closed-hand variant as well.
